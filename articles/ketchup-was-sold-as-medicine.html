Ketchup Was Sold as Medicine in the 1830s

📅 April 9, 2026 ⏱ 6 min read ✦ UselesslyInteresting

In this article

  2. Ketchup Before Tomatoes
  3. Other Foods That Were Once Medicine

The bottle of ketchup sitting in your fridge has a stranger history than you'd expect. For a brief but enthusiastic window in the 1830s, tomato extract was sold across America as a legitimate pharmaceutical treatment — bottled, pill-ified, and prescribed by actual doctors for actual patients.

Dr. Miles's Tomato Pills

In 1834, a physician named Dr. John Cook Bennett published a paper arguing that tomatoes could cure diarrhea, liver problems, indigestion, and bilious attacks (whatever those were). His claims spread rapidly through American medical journals, and within a few years, entrepreneurs were capitalizing on the craze.

The most famous product was "Dr. Miles' Compound Extract of Tomato" — a concentrated tomato extract sold in pill form, marketed as a cure for everything from dyspepsia to rheumatism. Pharmacies stocked it alongside laudanum and mercury-based tonics. Patients took it faithfully. Some reported feeling better, though this was almost certainly the placebo effect combined with the general nutritional benefit of actually eating a vegetable, which many 19th-century Americans were not doing nearly enough of.

The tomato pill craze faded by the 1850s as medical skeptics pushed back, but the underlying idea — that tomatoes had unusual health properties — wasn't entirely wrong. Tomatoes are rich in lycopene, a powerful antioxidant, and modern nutritional science does credit them with genuinely useful properties. Bennett was just wildly overpromising on the delivery system.

✦ Quick fact

In 1820, tomatoes were widely believed to be poisonous in America. The confusion came from their membership in the nightshade family. The moment when Colonel Robert Gibbon Johnson ate a basket of tomatoes on the steps of a courthouse in New Jersey — supposedly to prove they were safe — may be an apocryphal story, but the fear was absolutely real.

Ketchup Before Tomatoes

The word "ketchup" (also spelled catsup or catchup) comes from the Hokkien Chinese word "kê-tsiap," a fermented fish sauce brought to Southeast Asia and then to Europe by sailors in the 17th century. Early British ketchup recipes contained no tomatoes whatsoever — they were made from mushrooms, anchovies, walnuts, or oysters.

Mushroom ketchup was particularly popular in 18th-century Britain and can still be purchased today. Jane Austen mentions it in her letters. These early ketchups were dark, intensely savory, thin liquid condiments — closer to Worcestershire sauce than anything Heinz makes. Tomatoes didn't enter the ketchup equation until the early 1800s, when American cooks started substituting them into British mushroom ketchup recipes.

Other Foods That Were Once Medicine

Ketchup is not alone in its pharmaceutical past. Coca-Cola was originally sold in 1886 as a headache cure and nervous system tonic — the coca leaves in the original formula provided genuine cocaine. Graham crackers were invented by Sylvester Graham in the 1820s as part of a bland diet he believed would suppress sexual urges and cure all manner of moral and physical ills. Corn flakes emerged from a similar Puritan wellness philosophy promoted by John Harvey Kellogg at a Michigan sanitarium.

7-Up originally contained lithium citrate, a real mood-stabilizing compound, until 1950. Ginger ale was promoted as a stomach remedy. Tonic water, with its quinine content, was a genuine anti-malarial drink in tropical colonies — the gin was added to make it palatable, which is one of history's more enjoyable medical innovations.

✦ Quick fact

Heinz began selling bottled ketchup in 1876. Their formula was one of the first to add vinegar as a preservative, which transformed ketchup from a product that spoiled quickly into something shelf-stable. This was the real innovation that turned medicinal tomato extract into America's favorite condiment — not taste, but preservation chemistry.

When Medicine Became Condiment

The journey from pharmaceutical product to burger topping took about 50 years. As tomato ketchup became cheaper to produce and more widely available through the late 19th century, its identity shifted. By the early 1900s it was firmly a condiment, its medicinal past largely forgotten.

In 2012, the USDA briefly classified pizza sauce as a vegetable in school lunch programs — a decision mocked at the time, but one that echoes the same fuzzy line between food and medicine that tomatoes have straddled for two centuries. The tomato has never quite made up its mind what it wants to be. Fruit, vegetable, medicine, condiment. Perhaps that flexibility is why it won.
