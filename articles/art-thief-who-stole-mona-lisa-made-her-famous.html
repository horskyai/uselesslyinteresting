The Art Thief Who Stole the Mona Lisa — And Made Her Famous

📅 April 11, 2026 ⏱ 6 min read ✦ UselesslyInteresting

In this article

  2. The Worst Heist Ever Pulled Off
  3. The Two-Year Media Circus
  4. The Thief's Strange Patriotism

In August 1911, an Italian handyman named Vincenzo Peruggia walked out of the Louvre with Leonardo da Vinci's Mona Lisa hidden under his smock. He hid in a broom closet overnight, lifted the painting off its hook in the morning, and strolled out. What happened next is one of history's greatest accidents of fame.

Before the Theft, Nobody Cared

This is the part that genuinely surprises people: in 1911, the Mona Lisa was not considered the world's greatest painting. Art historians respected it; Leonardo da Vinci was already revered. But the Louvre hung it on a wall with no special protection, no prominent display, and no particular fanfare. Tourists walked past it.

A 1911 Baedeker guidebook to Paris — the tourist bible of the era — gave it a single star. The Raphael paintings nearby got more stars. The Mona Lisa was simply one excellent painting among thousands in a very large museum. It was famous among art professionals. It was not famous to the public. That distinction matters enormously for what came next.

✦ Quick fact

When the Mona Lisa went missing, the Louvre closed for an entire week. Upon reopening, more visitors came to stare at the empty hook where the painting had hung than had come to see the actual painting in years. The blank wall became a tourist attraction.

The Worst Heist Ever Pulled Off

Peruggia's method was embarrassingly simple. He had previously worked at the Louvre installing glass cases and knew the building's layout. On a Sunday night when the museum was closed, he hid inside and slept overnight in a storage closet. On Monday morning, before public hours, he lifted the Mona Lisa off its wall hooks — the painting wasn't bolted down — tucked it under his large work smock, and walked out a side door.

He carried it back to his Paris apartment, where it sat under his bed, wrapped in a red cloth, for two and a half years. He didn't sell it. He didn't ransom it. He just... had it. Investigators interviewed him at his apartment during the investigation and didn't find it — the painting was in a false-bottomed trunk the whole time.

The Two-Year Media Circus

The theft made every newspaper in the world. This was 1911: mass-circulation newspapers were the internet of their day, and an art theft from the world's most famous museum was extraordinary news. Suddenly millions of people who had never thought about the Mona Lisa were reading detailed descriptions of it, seeing reproductions of it, debating where it was.

The French government was embarrassed. Poet Guillaume Apollinaire was briefly arrested as a suspect. Pablo Picasso was interrogated. The investigation was a disaster of incompetence — police had Peruggia's fingerprints from the frame but couldn't match them because he had a prior record in Italy, not France.

By the time Peruggia was caught in 1913 — he was finally nabbed when he tried to sell the painting to a Florence art dealer who immediately called the police — the Mona Lisa had achieved something no museum marketing campaign could have manufactured: two years of sustained global public attention. When it was returned to the Louvre, crowds lined up for miles.

✦ Quick fact

Peruggia served only seven months in prison. At his trial, the Italian public largely sympathized with him — he had claimed he was reclaiming an Italian treasure stolen by Napoleon (which was historically inaccurate; Leonardo had voluntarily brought the painting to France). He was treated as something of a folk hero.

The Thief's Strange Patriotism

Peruggia genuinely believed he was doing Italy a service. He had convinced himself, incorrectly, that Napoleon had looted the Mona Lisa from Italy. In fact, Leonardo da Vinci himself had brought the painting to France when he moved there at the invitation of King Francis I in 1516. The Mona Lisa had been in France for nearly 400 years before Peruggia decided to "repatriate" it.

The historical irony is layered: a man who stole the painting based on a completely wrong understanding of history ended up performing the single greatest act of art publicity in history. The Mona Lisa's fame today — the bulletproof glass, the crowds fifty deep, the reproductions on a billion coffee mugs — traces directly back to a confused Italian workman who hid under his bed with a Renaissance masterpiece for two years. Leonardo spent four years painting it. Peruggia made it eternal in twenty-four hours.
