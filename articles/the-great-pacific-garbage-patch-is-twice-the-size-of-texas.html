The Great Pacific Garbage Patch Is Twice the Size of Texas

Somewhere between Hawaii and California, in a stretch of ocean larger than the entire country of Mexico, there is a mass of plastic debris that did not exist fifty years ago. It cannot be seen from space — it is not a solid island but a diffuse soup of fragments, fibers, and ghost gear suspended in blue water. It is invisible until you sail into it. And then it goes on for weeks.

What follows is the story of how humanity turned the world's largest ocean into a plastic sink — and what that means for every creature, including us, that depends on the sea.

01

What the Garbage Patch Actually Looks Like

The name "Great Pacific Garbage Patch" conjures an image of a floating continent of bottles and bags, a solid mass you could walk across. The reality is simultaneously less dramatic and more disturbing. Sail into the patch and what you see is water — vast, blue, and deceptively normal-looking. But haul a fine mesh net through it and examine what you collect, and the picture changes completely.

The patch is better described as a plastic smog than a plastic island. The majority of its mass is composed of particles smaller than a grain of rice — microplastics, the disintegrated remnants of larger objects that have been broken down by UV radiation, wave action, and temperature cycling over years and decades. These particles are suspended throughout the water column, not just floating on the surface. They are invisible to the naked eye until you look closely and carefully, and then they are everywhere.

The larger objects are present too — fishing nets, ropes, buoys, bottles, bags, crates, and the recognizable detritus of human civilization — but they are distributed across an area so vast that you can sail for hours without encountering a visible piece. The patch's defining characteristic is not density but extent. It covers an estimated 1.6 million square kilometers — roughly twice the area of Texas, or three times the area of France.

The Ocean Cleanup project's 2018 study, the most comprehensive survey of the patch to date, estimated that it contains approximately 80,000 metric tons of plastic. This figure sounds manageable until you consider what it represents: 1.8 trillion individual plastic pieces, ranging from large objects to microplastic particles, concentrated in a single oceanic region. And that's just one of five major ocean garbage accumulation zones.

The Great Pacific Garbage Patch is not a solid island. It is an invisible smog of 1.8 trillion plastic pieces spread across an area twice the size of Texas. You can sail through it for days without seeing it — until you look closely. Then it is everywhere.

02

How Ocean Gyres Trap Plastic for Decades

To understand why plastic accumulates in the middle of the Pacific Ocean — far from any coastline, far from any obvious source — you need to understand ocean gyres. A gyre is a large system of rotating ocean currents driven by global wind patterns and the Coriolis effect (the deflection caused by Earth's rotation). There are five major subtropical gyres on Earth, one in each of the major ocean basins. Each one rotates continuously, drawing surface water and anything floating in it toward its calm, high-pressure center.

The North Pacific Subtropical Gyre, which hosts the Great Pacific Garbage Patch, rotates clockwise and spans much of the northern Pacific Ocean. Its center is a region of relatively still, stable water where convergent currents slow and surface material accumulates. Plastic enters from coastal sources thousands of kilometers away — carried by rivers, blown offshore from beaches, lost from ships — and gradually spirals inward over months and years until it reaches the convergence zone at the gyre's heart.

Once plastic reaches the convergence zone, it tends to stay. The circulation patterns that carried it there now trap it. UV radiation slowly breaks larger pieces into smaller ones, but the material doesn't leave. It just becomes smaller and more dispersed. A plastic bottle that enters the ocean off the coast of Japan today may still be in the North Pacific Gyre in fifty years — not as a bottle, but as thousands of microscopic fragments indistinguishable from plankton to the filter-feeders that will inevitably consume them.

The residence time of plastic in ocean gyres is measured in decades to centuries. Common plastics like polyethylene and polypropylene have estimated ocean degradation timelines of 450 to 1,000 years for complete breakdown into carbon dioxide and water — if they ever fully break down at all under oceanic conditions. No one knows for certain because plastic pollution is newer than the research programs studying it.

Ocean gyres are nature's conveyor belts — and they deliver plastic from every coast to their calm, trapping centers. Once there, it stays. A bottle lost at sea today will still be circulating as microplastic fragments a century from now.

03

How Much Plastic Is in the Ocean?

The honest answer is: we don't know precisely, and the more carefully scientists look, the worse it gets.

The most widely cited estimate, from a 2015 study by Jambeck and colleagues published in Science, calculated that approximately 8 million metric tons of plastic enter the ocean every year. That figure was based on waste generation data from 192 coastal countries and estimates of mismanagement rates. To put it in physical terms: it is roughly equivalent to dumping a garbage truck full of plastic into the ocean every minute of every day.

Since that estimate was published, subsequent research has consistently suggested it was conservative. A 2021 study in Science Advances estimated that the total plastic currently present in the ocean — on the surface, in the water column, and on the seafloor — may already exceed 150 million metric tons. The seafloor, it turns out, is a far larger plastic sink than the surface patches: deep-sea surveys have found plastic debris in the Mariana Trench, the deepest point on Earth, at depths exceeding 10,000 meters.

The discrepancy between the amount of plastic entering the ocean each year and the amount found in surface surveys has long puzzled researchers. Where is all the plastic going? The emerging answer is: down. Microplastic particles sink as they become colonized by biofilm communities of bacteria and algae that increase their density. Larger items are dragged to the bottom by physical processes. The ocean surface we can sample represents only a small fraction of total ocean plastic inventory.

Production rates make the problem exponentially worse. Global plastic production has increased from roughly 2 million metric tons in 1950 to over 400 million metric tons per year today. Less than 10% of all plastic ever produced has been recycled. The remainder has been landfilled, incinerated, or — in substantial quantities — leaked into the environment. Unless production and waste management practices change dramatically, ocean plastic inputs are projected to triple by 2040.

8 million metric tons of plastic enter the ocean every year — a garbage truck per minute. Over 150 million metric tons already sit in the ocean, most of it on the seafloor. And production is accelerating. The numbers keep getting worse the more carefully anyone measures.

04

Plastic Never Disappears — It Only Gets Smaller

The most important thing to understand about plastic pollution is that plastic does not biodegrade in any meaningful sense. When plastic is exposed to sunlight, heat, and mechanical abrasion in the ocean, it undergoes photodegradation — UV radiation breaks the polymer chains that give plastic its structure, causing it to become brittle and fragment. But fragmentation is not degradation. The polymer molecules are still there. They have just become smaller.

This process continues indefinitely, at least on human timescales. Macroplastics (objects larger than 5 cm) break into mesoplastics (5 mm to 5 cm), which break into microplastics (less than 5 mm), which break into nanoplastics (less than 1 micrometer). At nanoplastic scale, the particles are invisible to the naked eye, capable of crossing biological membranes, and impossible to remove from the ocean with any current technology. They are, for all practical purposes, permanent.

The proliferation of microplastics is now so extensive that researchers have begun using microplastic concentration as a stratigraphic marker in marine sediment cores — a way of dating sediment layers the way geologists use isotopes. The plastic signal appears sharply in the mid-20th century and intensifies rapidly through the 1990s and 2000s. Future geologists, examining ocean sediment cores, will see our era as a distinct geological layer defined by synthetic polymer particles. We are literally leaving plastic in the rock record.

Some plastic additives — plasticizers, flame retardants, UV stabilizers, colorants — leach out of plastic fragments as they degrade and dissolve into surrounding seawater. Many of these additives are persistent organic pollutants with known endocrine-disrupting, carcinogenic, or neurotoxic properties. Plastic fragments also act as sponges, adsorbing other persistent pollutants like PCBs and DDT from surrounding seawater and concentrating them at levels orders of magnitude higher than ambient seawater. When marine animals ingest plastic, they ingest this concentrated cocktail of chemical contaminants along with it.

Plastic doesn't break down — it breaks up. Every piece ever made still exists in some form. At nanoscale, it's invisible, impossible to collect, and capable of crossing cell membranes. We have created an immortal pollutant and released it into every ocean on Earth.

05

The Animals Eating Our Garbage

In 1997, a researcher named Charles Moore was sailing his catamaran back from a race in Hawaii to California when he decided to take a shortcut through the North Pacific Subtropical Gyre. For a week, no matter which direction he looked, he saw plastic debris. "In the week it took to cross the subtropical high," he later wrote, "no matter what time of day I looked, plastic debris was floating everywhere."

Moore went on to found the Algalita Marine Research and Education foundation and dedicate his life to studying ocean plastic. What he and subsequent researchers have documented about the impact on marine wildlife is one of the most comprehensive indictments of human industrial behavior ever assembled.

Seabirds are among the most visible victims. Albatrosses, which skim the ocean surface catching squid and fish, cannot distinguish plastic debris from food. Adult albatrosses feed plastic to their chicks on Midway Atoll in the North Pacific — thousands of kilometers from any continental coastline — and researchers have found chick carcasses with stomachs containing bottle caps, cigarette lighters, toy soldiers, and plastic bags. An estimated 90% of all seabirds alive today have ingested plastic at some point, according to a 2015 study in PNAS. That figure is projected to reach 99% by 2050 if current trends continue.

Sea turtles mistake plastic bags for jellyfish, their primary prey. A 2018 analysis found that sea turtles that had ingested just 14 pieces of plastic had a 50% chance of death. Ingested plastic causes intestinal blockages, perforations, false satiety (the turtle feels full without having consumed any nutrition), and chemical poisoning from the additives and adsorbed contaminants the plastic carries.

Whales and dolphins strand on beaches with stomachs packed with plastic. In 2019, a Cuvier's beaked whale stranded in the Philippines was found to have 40 kilograms of plastic bags in its stomach. In 2018, a sperm whale that stranded on a Spanish beach had 29 kilograms of plastic waste blocking its digestive system. These are not isolated incidents; they are documented with increasing regularity on coastlines worldwide.

Even creatures that don't obviously eat plastic are affected. Coral reefs become entangled in plastic debris, which blocks light, abrades the coral's delicate polyps, and introduces bacterial pathogens. A 2018 study published in Science found that coral colonies in contact with plastic had an 89% chance of being diseased, compared to 4% for plastic-free colonies.

90% of all seabirds alive today have eaten plastic. Sea turtles die after ingesting just 14 pieces. Whales strand with kilograms of bags in their stomachs. We have made plastic so ubiquitous that it has become part of the diet of virtually every marine animal on Earth.

06

Microplastics Have Entered the Food Chain

For years, the concern about plastic pollution focused primarily on the visible: the images of turtles entangled in fishing line, the seabirds with stomachs full of bottle caps. But the more alarming story is happening at a scale too small to see. Microplastics are now so pervasive in marine environments that they have entered the food chain at its base, and the implications for every organism in the ocean — including humans — are only beginning to be understood.

Zooplankton, the microscopic animals that form the foundation of marine food webs, are ingesting microplastic particles. Studies have shown that microplastic ingestion reduces zooplankton feeding rates, reproduction, and survival. When zooplankton are compromised, the entire ocean food web is affected: the fish that eat zooplankton, the larger fish that eat those fish, the marine mammals and seabirds that eat those larger fish, and ultimately the humans who eat seafood.

The contamination of seafood with microplastics is now well documented. A 2017 study found microplastics in 83% of shrimp samples tested from markets across the world. Mussels, oysters, and other filter feeders concentrate microplastics from surrounding water and pass them directly to consumers. A person who eats seafood regularly is consuming an estimated 11,000 to 130,000 microplastic particles per year, according to research published in Environmental Science & Technology.

The health implications of this chronic low-level microplastic ingestion are a subject of intense ongoing research. What is already established: microplastics and the chemical additives they carry can cause oxidative stress and inflammation in cells, disrupt hormone signaling (many plastic additives are endocrine disruptors), and potentially transport pathogens to unusual anatomical locations. Nanoplastics, small enough to cross cell membranes, have been detected in human blood, lung tissue, and placentas. Whether chronic exposure causes measurable health harm in humans remains under investigation — but the precautionary principle suggests that having immortal synthetic polymer particles and their chemical cargo distributed throughout your body tissues is not beneficial.

Microplastics have entered the base of the marine food chain. Seafood eaters consume up to 130,000 plastic particles per year. Nanoplastics have been found in human blood, lungs, and placentas. We do not yet know the health consequences. We do know the particles are there.

07

Ghost Gear: The Fishing Nets That Never Stop Fishing

Of all the plastic in the ocean, perhaps none is more actively destructive than abandoned, lost, or discarded fishing gear — what the ocean conservation community calls "ghost gear." The Ocean Cleanup's 2018 analysis of the Great Pacific Garbage Patch found that approximately 46% of its plastic mass by weight consisted of fishing nets, with the remainder being a mixture of other fishing-related items and general consumer plastic debris.

Ghost nets are among the most dangerous objects in the ocean because they continue doing what they were designed to do: catch marine animals. A drifting net, freed from its floats and weights, sinks until it catches enough marine life to provide buoyancy, then rises to the surface where UV and wave action degrade some of the trapped organisms, reducing the load, causing the net to sink again, and restarting the cycle. This "ghost fishing" cycle can continue for years or decades, entangling and killing fish, sea turtles, dolphins, seals, and seabirds continuously.

The World Animal Protection organization estimates that ghost gear kills over 100,000 whales, dolphins, seals, and sea lions per year, along with countless fish and seabirds. A single abandoned net can be tens of kilometers in length. In 2020, divers off the coast of southern California discovered and removed a ghost net measuring over a kilometer long, weighing nearly three tons, and containing the remains of more than forty marine mammals.

Ghost gear originates primarily from commercial fishing operations. Gear is lost in storms, abandoned because retrieval is too costly, or deliberately discarded. Regulations requiring gear marking and mandating retrieval of lost equipment exist in many jurisdictions but are poorly enforced, particularly on the high seas. The economics of fishing frequently make abandoning gear cheaper than recovering it.

Some fishing communities and conservation organizations have launched gear retrieval programs that pay fishing boats to recover abandoned nets from the ocean. In Alaska, a program run by the NOAA Marine Debris Program has removed over 3,000 metric tons of derelict fishing gear from the North Pacific since 2002. These efforts are meaningful but operate at a tiny fraction of the scale of the problem.

Ghost nets make up 46% of the Great Pacific Garbage Patch by weight. They keep fishing indefinitely, killing over 100,000 marine mammals per year. A single abandoned net can be a kilometer long and kill for decades. The ocean's most active killer is invisible, drifting, and entirely man-made.

08

Where Does All This Plastic Come From?

One of the most common misconceptions about ocean plastic is that it primarily comes from ships and offshore platforms dumping waste at sea. Ship-source pollution is real and significant, but the dominant source of ocean plastic is land-based, delivered primarily by rivers.

A landmark 2017 study published in Nature Communications by Schmidt and colleagues estimated that between 1.15 and 2.41 million metric tons of plastic enter the ocean from rivers each year. The study identified the major contributors: ten rivers account for approximately 88% to 95% of riverine plastic input to the oceans. Eight of those ten rivers are in Asia, with the Yangtze River in China estimated to be the single largest contributor. The Ganges in India, the Xi, Dong, and Zhujiang in China, the Indus in Pakistan, and several Southeast Asian rivers complete the list.

This geographic concentration reflects the intersection of high plastic consumption, inadequate waste management infrastructure, and high river discharge. In many rapidly developing nations, plastic consumption has grown faster than the infrastructure to manage plastic waste safely. Plastic that isn't collected ends up in waterways, blown into drainage channels, or deposited on riverbanks where floods carry it to sea.

This is not simply a problem of irresponsible behavior by individuals in developing countries — a framing that wealthy nations frequently deploy to deflect attention from their own role. The United States and European countries export enormous quantities of plastic waste to developing nations, ostensibly for recycling. Much of this exported waste ends up in waterways. The UK, for instance, exported over half a million metric tons of plastic waste in 2019, much of it to countries with limited recycling capacity. Plastic pollution is, in part, an externalized cost of wealthy-nation consumption patterns.

Single-use plastic items dominate the type profile of ocean plastic: bags, bottles, straws, food packaging, cigarette filters (which are plastic), and foam containers. These are products designed to be used once and discarded, with a useful life measured in minutes and a marine residence time measured in centuries.

90% of ocean plastic arrives via rivers, mostly in Asia — but wealthy nations export their plastic waste there. Single-use items designed for minutes of use persist in the ocean for centuries. The problem is global, the sources are human, and responsibility is distributed across every nation that produces and consumes plastic.

09

The Other Four Garbage Patches Nobody Talks About

The Great Pacific Garbage Patch is the most studied and most discussed accumulation zone, but it is not unique. Every major ocean gyre accumulates plastic, and there are five subtropical gyres on Earth. The North Atlantic Garbage Patch, South Pacific Garbage Patch, South Atlantic Garbage Patch, and Indian Ocean Garbage Patch all exist, are all growing, and receive far less scientific and media attention than their Pacific counterpart.

The North Atlantic Garbage Patch, located between Europe and North America, has been estimated to contain over 200,000 metric tons of plastic. It has been accumulating debris since at least the 1970s, and researchers have noted that its concentration of microplastic is comparable to that of the North Pacific patch. The Sargasso Sea, a region of the North Atlantic defined by the surrounding gyres and famous for its floating mats of Sargassum seaweed, is particularly heavily contaminated — the seaweed mats trap plastic debris, concentrating it in a biologically rich habitat that numerous species depend on for shelter and breeding.

The Indian Ocean Garbage Patch is of particular concern because the Indian Ocean receives runoff from some of the world's most densely populated coastlines, including the east coast of Africa, the Indian subcontinent, Southeast Asia, and Australia. It is also one of the least monitored ocean regions for plastic debris, meaning that estimates of its size and content are less reliable than for the Pacific patches.

In addition to the five major gyre-based accumulation zones, plastic debris has been documented in the Arctic Ocean, where it arrives via ocean currents from the Atlantic and Pacific. A 2017 study found microplastics in Arctic sea ice at concentrations higher than in surface waters of many ocean gyres, suggesting that sea ice acts as a temporary sink for microplastics that will be released as climate change reduces Arctic ice coverage.

There are five garbage patches, not one. The Great Pacific is merely the most famous. The North Atlantic, Indian Ocean, South Pacific, and South Atlantic patches are all growing. Plastic has even been found concentrated in Arctic sea ice — waiting to be released as the ice melts.

10

Can We Clean the Ocean?

Since the Great Pacific Garbage Patch entered public consciousness in the mid-2000s, dozens of proposals to clean it up have been advanced. Some are serious engineering projects; others are wishful thinking dressed in technical language. The honest assessment of ocean plastic cleanup efforts is: useful at the margins, fundamentally insufficient without upstream reductions.

The Ocean Cleanup project, founded by Dutch entrepreneur Boyan Slat in 2013, has received the most attention and funding. Its System 003 — a 2.2-kilometer-long floating barrier with a below-surface screen that allows fish to pass beneath while trapping plastic — collected over 10,000 kilograms of plastic from the North Pacific Gyre in its first full operational year. This is genuinely meaningful. It is also roughly 0.013% of the estimated 80,000 metric tons in the patch. At that rate, cleaning the North Pacific Garbage Patch alone would take approximately 6,000 years.

The deeper problem is that surface collection systems cannot capture microplastics, which constitute the majority of plastic particles in the ocean by count. Microplastics are too small and too dispersed to be economically collected from open ocean. Any system fine-meshed enough to capture them would also trap zooplankton and other marine life at catastrophic rates. The ocean microplastic problem, as currently understood, has no technological solution. Preventing microplastic inputs is the only viable strategy.

Riverine interception — collecting plastic from rivers before it reaches the sea — is significantly more tractable than open-ocean collection. Organizations like The Ocean Cleanup have deployed "Interceptor" systems in major plastic-contributing rivers in Indonesia, Malaysia, Vietnam, and other Southeast Asian nations. These systems can remove substantial quantities of plastic before it reaches the ocean, where it becomes progressively harder to collect. But again, they operate at a small fraction of the scale of annual plastic inputs.

Beach cleanup efforts, pursued globally by millions of volunteers, remove significant quantities of plastic from coastal areas and prevent some ocean entry. They also generate valuable data on plastic type and source. But the ocean's vastness and plastic's longevity mean that cleanup, however vigorous, is a rearguard action without a fundamental change in production and disposal patterns.

The Ocean Cleanup project is real, impressive, and collects 0.013% of the plastic in the North Pacific patch per year. Microplastics cannot be removed from open ocean without killing marine life. The only way to fix the ocean plastic problem is to stop putting plastic in it.

11

The Plastic Industry's War on Recycling

In 2019, investigative journalists at NPR and PBS Frontline obtained internal documents from the plastic industry dating back to the 1970s. What they revealed was one of the most consequential deceptions in environmental history: for decades, the plastic industry promoted recycling as the solution to plastic pollution while privately acknowledging that most plastic recycling was economically unviable and functionally impossible.

The documents showed that industry executives knew by the 1970s that the economics of plastic recycling were fundamentally problematic. Virgin plastic is cheap to produce. Collected and sorted plastic is expensive to process. The end product — recycled resin — is often inferior in quality to virgin material and costs more to produce. Without subsidies or mandates, the economics almost never work. Mechanical recycling is also limited by material compatibility: the many different types of plastic (PET, HDPE, PVC, LDPE, PP, PS, and others) cannot be recycled together, requiring costly sorting infrastructure.

Despite knowing this, the industry invested heavily in promoting the recyclability of plastic to forestall regulation. The familiar "chasing arrows" recycling symbol was placed on virtually all plastic products, regardless of whether any recycling infrastructure existed for them. The symbol did not mean the product would be recycled — it meant it was theoretically recyclable under ideal conditions. Most consumers did not understand this distinction, and the industry did not go out of its way to clarify it.

The result: of the approximately 9.2 billion metric tons of plastic produced since 1950, only about 9% has ever been recycled. Approximately 12% has been incinerated. The remaining 79% has accumulated in landfills or leaked into the natural environment. The recycling system was not a solution. It was a narrative designed to make plastic pollution the consumer's problem rather than the producer's responsibility.

Only 9% of all plastic ever made has been recycled. The industry knew recycling was unviable for decades and promoted it anyway to prevent regulation. The recycling symbol on your plastic packaging is not a promise — it is a misdirection.

12

What Actually Works: Reducing Plastic at the Source

The history of environmental policy contains numerous examples of pollution problems that seemed intractable until a political decision was made to simply stop producing the pollutant. Lead was removed from gasoline not by developing better lead-removal technologies, but by phasing out leaded fuel. CFCs were eliminated from refrigerants and aerosols not by developing better atmospheric scrubbing systems, but by mandating alternative chemicals. The lesson for plastic pollution is clear: the most effective solution is reduction at the source.

Single-use plastic bans have been implemented in over 120 countries in various forms. The European Union's Single-Use Plastics Directive, which came into effect in 2021, banned the ten most common single-use plastic items found on European beaches: cotton bud sticks, cutlery, plates, straws, stirrers, sticks for balloons, cups, food containers, cigarette filters, and plastic bags. Early data from member states indicates measurable reductions in these items in coastal surveys.

Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) schemes, which require plastic manufacturers to fund the collection and recycling of the plastic they produce, have shown promise in some jurisdictions. The theory is that if producers bear the full lifecycle cost of their products, they have an economic incentive to design for recyclability, use less material, and develop genuinely circular business models. Several European countries have implemented EPR for packaging with measurable reductions in packaging waste.

The most ambitious global response to date is the UN Global Plastics Treaty, negotiations for which began in 2022 and aimed for a legally binding agreement by 2024. The treaty, if adopted with meaningful commitments, could establish global caps on plastic production, mandate minimum recycled content, eliminate the most problematic plastic types, and establish binding timelines for phase-outs of single-use plastics. Its ultimate scope and enforceability remain subject to intense negotiation, with major petrochemical-producing nations resisting production caps.

What individuals can meaningfully do is support policies and purchase choices that reduce plastic production rather than managing plastic waste after it exists. Avoiding single-use plastic when alternatives are available, supporting businesses with genuine plastic reduction commitments, and voting for politicians who support binding plastic production regulation creates the demand signal that makes systemic change possible. The ocean doesn't need better cleanup technology. It needs less plastic entering it.

Lead pollution was solved by removing lead from gasoline. Ozone depletion was solved by banning CFCs. Ocean plastic will be solved by reducing plastic production — not by better recycling or cleanup technology. The solution exists. It requires political will, not innovation.

The Great Pacific Garbage Patch did not exist before the mid-20th century. It was created, in its entirety, by human decisions: the decision to design single-use products with no viable end-of-life plan, the decision to let producers externalize the cost of waste management, the decision to export waste rather than reduce it, and the collective decision to treat the ocean as a sink with infinite capacity to absorb whatever we chose to discard.

The ocean's capacity is not infinite. The evidence for this is already visible in the bodies of seabirds that feed their chicks bottle caps, in the whales that strand with stomachs packed with bags, in the microplastics accumulating in human placentas, and in the trillion-piece plastic smog spread across two Texas-sized patches of the Pacific.

None of this is inevitable. Single-use plastic has existed for roughly seventy years. The ocean has existed for roughly four billion years. The question is whether we treat the next seventy years the way we treated the last seventy, or whether we decide that an immortal pollutant saturating every marine food chain and entering the tissues of every animal that eats seafood — including us — represents a sufficient reason to do something different.

The patch will still be there tomorrow. The question is whether it's larger or smaller.
