The Wow! Signal — The Only Possible Alien Message Ever Received

April 7, 2026 • 6 min read

Table of Contents

  5. Why It Has Never Repeated

The printout still exists. It is a strip of computer paper from 1977, covered in rows of numbers and letters that represent radio frequency data collected by the Big Ear telescope at Ohio State University. In the middle of the printout, someone has circled a cluster of characters in red pen — "6EQUJ5" — and written next to them, in neat capital letters: "Wow!"

That annotation, made by astronomer Jerry Ehman on August 15, 1977, gave the most compelling candidate for an extraterrestrial radio signal in the history of SETI its enduring name. Nearly fifty years later, the signal remains unexplained.

01

The Night It Happened

The Big Ear radio telescope at Ohio State University was not the most sophisticated instrument of its era, but it was dedicated. Opened in 1963, it was a fixed telescope that could not be steered — instead, it relied on Earth's rotation to sweep the sky, scanning a strip of the celestial sphere as the planet turned beneath it. This design made it reliable for long-term monitoring projects, and it had been participating in the Search for Extraterrestrial Intelligence (SETI) since 1973.

On August 15, 1977, at 11:16pm EDT, as Big Ear scanned a region of sky in the constellation Sagittarius, it detected a radio signal at a frequency of approximately 1420.4556 megahertz. The telescope recorded data on paper printout, with signal intensity represented by numbers (0-9) and then letters (A-Z, representing 10-35 times the background noise level). The Wow! Signal's peak intensity read "U" — 30 times background noise.

Jerry Ehman was reviewing the printout several days later when he noticed the sequence. The pattern of intensity rise, peak, and fall over 72 seconds matched precisely the expected profile of a genuine extraterrestrial signal coming from a fixed point in space as Earth's rotation carried the telescope beam across it. The signal appeared in one of Big Ear's two feed horns (it would have appeared in both if it were terrestrial interference), further suggesting it came from space.

Ehman was careful in his initial reaction, later explaining that "Wow!" was an expression of surprise at the signal's unusualness, not a claim about its origin. He spent years looking for alternative explanations before concluding that he could find none that fully accounted for the signal's characteristics. "The Wow! Signal," he wrote in 1997, "remains the strongest candidate for an extraterrestrial radio signal ever detected."

Ehman's "Wow!" was a scientist's expression of surprise, not a claim about aliens. He spent years looking for a better explanation. Nearly fifty years later, he never found one that fully worked. The signal remains officially unexplained.

02

What Made It So Strange

To understand why the Wow! Signal attracted such sustained attention, it helps to understand what SETI researchers were looking for and why this signal matched the profile so well. Since Frank Drake's pioneering work in the 1960s, SETI had been predicated on the idea that an advanced technological civilization capable of interstellar communication might broadcast radio waves at a specific frequency: the 21-centimeter hydrogen line (approximately 1420 megahertz).

The hydrogen line was chosen because it is the radio frequency at which neutral hydrogen atoms naturally emit and absorb radiation. Hydrogen is the most abundant element in the universe. Any civilization with basic knowledge of physics would be aware of this frequency, and the reasoning went that a deliberate signal intended for other civilizations would be broadcast near this frequency as a kind of universal calling card.

The Wow! Signal came in at 1420.4556 megahertz — within the hydrogen line region. This alone was striking. But several other characteristics made it additionally unusual. Its strength — 30 times background noise — was far above any natural astrophysical source known to produce narrow-band radio emission. It appeared to come from a fixed point in space (as opposed to terrestrial sources, which move relative to the telescope). It lasted exactly 72 seconds, consistent with a source tracked by Earth's rotation for the duration Big Ear's beam would remain on a fixed celestial position. And it appeared in only one of the telescope's two receiver horns — consistent with a distant space source, inconsistent with local interference.

The Wow! Signal matched the theoretical profile of an intentional alien broadcast on almost every measurable dimension: frequency, strength, duration, and directionality. The match was not partial. It was nearly perfect. That is what made it so difficult to dismiss.

03

The Search for an Explanation

In the decades since 1977, the Wow! Signal has been the subject of persistent investigation, multiple targeted follow-up observations, and numerous proposed natural explanations — none of which has achieved scientific consensus as a complete account of what happened.

The most immediately attractive alternative explanation was terrestrial radio frequency interference (RFI) — a signal from a human-made source such as a satellite, aircraft, or ground-based transmitter that happened to match the expected signature. But the signal's appearance in only one receiver horn, combined with the frequency (which is internationally protected from terrestrial broadcasting by treaty), makes RFI a poor fit. The internationally coordinated spectrum protection means the 1420 MHz hydrogen line band is essentially radio-silent from human sources under normal conditions.

Astrophysical natural sources have also been proposed: pulsars, interstellar hydrogen masers, or radio bursts from flare stars. But pulsars produce repeating signals at regular intervals, not 72-second one-off events. Hydrogen masers can produce strong narrow-band emission at 1420 MHz, but at intensities far below what was observed, and typically from extended regions of the sky rather than a point source. Fast Radio Bursts, a class of astrophysical transient discovered in 2007, are too brief (milliseconds) and too broadband to explain a 72-second narrow-band event.

Ohio State University directed Big Ear to re-observe the region of sky from which the signal appeared to originate over 50 times in the years following 1977. The signal was never detected again. Multiple other radio telescopes have since surveyed the same sky region without detecting anything unusual.

No known natural astrophysical source fits the Wow! Signal's profile. No human-made interference source fits it either. Over fifty re-observations of the same sky region found nothing. The signal appeared once, matched every expected criterion for an alien broadcast, and vanished.

04

The Comets Theory — And Why It's Contested

In 2017, astronomer Antonio Paris proposed a new natural explanation for the Wow! Signal: two comets, 266P/Christensen and 335P/Gibbs, which were in approximately the right region of sky in August 1977, could have produced hydrogen emission at 1420 MHz from their hydrogen coma — the cloud of hydrogen gas surrounding a comet's nucleus.

The proposal attracted attention because it offered a specific, testable natural explanation for the first time. Paris observed 266P/Christensen with a radio telescope in 2016-2017 and reported detecting 1420 MHz emission from it, suggesting comets could indeed produce the necessary signal.

However, the comet hypothesis has been contested by other researchers, including Ehman himself. Critics point out that the Wow! Signal's intensity was far stronger than any cometary hydrogen emission subsequently observed. Additionally, the hydrogen coma of a comet is spatially extended — it would appear as a diffuse source spread across multiple beam widths, not the point-like source that matched Big Ear's single-horn detection. The comet hypothesis also does not explain why, if comets routinely produce such signals, similar detections have not been made by the many radio telescopes operating since 1977.

The scientific consensus, to the extent one exists, is that the comet hypothesis is an interesting proposal that has not overcome the objections raised against it. The Wow! Signal remains unexplained by natural causes that fit all of its characteristics simultaneously.

The comets hypothesis was a genuine scientific attempt to explain the Wow! Signal naturally. It was tested. The critics who examined it found it didn't fit all the data. The signal remains without a satisfactory explanation, natural or otherwise.

05

Why It Has Never Repeated

The failure of the Wow! Signal to repeat is both its most frustrating characteristic and one of the most discussed aspects of the SETI community's interpretation of it. Scientific evidence requires reproducibility. A signal detected once, never to be seen again, cannot be confirmed or studied in any conventional sense. Even if the Wow! Signal were of extraterrestrial technological origin, its non-recurrence creates severe problems for drawing any conclusions.

SETI researchers have proposed several frameworks for understanding why a genuinely artificial alien signal might not repeat. One is the "lighthouse" model: a highly directional beam sweeping through the galaxy, like a lighthouse beam, which would only intersect with Earth's position occasionally. If the signal source was sweeping the galactic plane in a broad search, it might illuminate Earth's position for 72 seconds and then not return for decades, centuries, or longer.

Another framework involves the extraordinary distances involved. The region of sky from which the Wow! Signal appears to have originated is approximately 120 to 1,000 light-years away, in the direction of Sagittarius. A round-trip communication at light speed from a civilization at 120 light-years would take 240 years. There is simply no way to ask for a repeat and receive an answer in any practical timeframe.

A third and more prosaic possibility: the signal was a one-time event even by the standards of its source. Sporadic astrophysical phenomena produce one-time bursts. An alien civilization conducting a brief experiment or test transmission might not repeat it on any schedule useful to observers on Earth. Without the ability to direct a reply and wait centuries for a response, SETI is in the position of listening to a radio that plays one song, once, and then goes silent.

A signal that never repeats cannot be confirmed as real. But SETI researchers point out that an alien lighthouse sweeping the galaxy might point at Earth for 72 seconds every few decades. We've only been listening carefully for 60 years. We might have caught one sweep.

06

What the Wow! Signal Says About the Search for Life

The Wow! Signal occupies a unique position in the history of SETI: it is the closest thing to confirmed extraterrestrial contact that has ever been recorded, and it is also, simultaneously, conclusive proof of nothing. This paradox says something important about the search for life in the universe and the difficulty of knowing what we are looking for before we find it.

SETI has now been operating for approximately six decades, and the Wow! Signal remains its most anomalous result. Tens of thousands of hours of radio telescope observation have found nothing else that matches it. This could mean that the signal was natural and unusual. It could mean that technological civilizations are extraordinarily rare. It could mean that we are searching in the wrong way — that a civilization capable of interstellar communication would not use radio waves, or would not broadcast at 1420 MHz, or would not broadcast in any way we know how to detect.

In 2012, a group of researchers sent a response to the approximate region of the sky from which the Wow! Signal came — a digital message containing prime number sequences and other mathematical constructs likely to be recognizable as artificial. The response was transmitted from the Arecibo Observatory. If the signal came from a source approximately 120 light-years away, a response to our reply could arrive around 2362. We have left a message in a bottle pointed at a region of sky that may contain nothing at all. Or may contain something that will one day answer.

The Wow! Signal endures as a scientific mystery because it combines extraordinary specificity — it matched every expected criterion for what an alien signal would look like — with extraordinary impermanence. It appeared, matched, and vanished. It invited the most profound question in science: are we alone? And then it offered no answer.

A response to the Wow! Signal was sent in 2012. If the source is 120 light-years away, a reply could arrive in 2362. We might have said something to something. We have no way to know. We have to wait 336 years to find out if anyone writes back.

The original printout with Ehman's "Wow!" annotation is preserved. The question it poses — a handwritten exclamation on a strip of computer paper from 1977 — remains open. In the silence of the cosmos, something spoke once, matched every criterion of being deliberate, and has not spoken since.

That might mean nothing. Or it might be the most significant thing that has ever happened to the human species, still waiting to be understood.
