Bull Sharks Have Friends — And They Choose Them More Carefully Than You Do

March 23, 2026 • 18 min read

For decades, we've been told that sharks are mindless killing machines — cold-eyed loners cruising the ocean with nothing on their agenda but the next meal. Turns out, bull sharks not only have friends, they pick them with more deliberation than most humans swipe right. A six-year study just blew the lid off everything we thought we knew about shark social life. And honestly? It's making us look bad.

A University of Exeter research team spent six years in Fiji tracking 184 bull sharks. What they found changes everything.

01

Everything You Think About Sharks Is Wrong

Let's start with a confession that the entire field of marine biology has been quietly making for years: we got sharks wrong. Spectacularly, embarrassingly, Hollywood-level wrong.

Ask anyone to describe a shark and you'll get some variation of the same answer: a solitary predator, dead-eyed and emotionless, driven by nothing more than a primitive hunger circuit that hasn't been updated since the Cretaceous. It's the animal equivalent of a serial killer — no friends, no feelings, no social skills. Just teeth and momentum.

This image is so deeply embedded in popular culture that it feels like biological fact. It isn't. It never was. And a study published on March 17, 2026 in the journal Animal Behaviour just delivered the most comprehensive demolition of the "sharks are loners" myth ever produced.

The study, led by Professor Darren Croft at the University of Exeter, tracked 184 bull sharks over six years in Fiji. They analyzed social networks. They mapped friendships. They documented who hangs out with whom, who avoids whom, and who's the popular kid in the shark equivalent of a high school cafeteria.

The results? Bull sharks have active social preferences. They choose specific individuals to spend time with. They avoid others. They have, for lack of a less human word, friends.

And before you dismiss this as anthropomorphism run amok, consider the methodology: six years of data, nearly two hundred individual animals, rigorous statistical analysis of association patterns. This isn't a feel-good puff piece about a shark that seemed to smile at a diver. This is peer-reviewed science saying that one of the ocean's most feared predators maintains a social life that is, in many ways, more deliberate than yours.

02

The Jaws Problem: How Pop Culture Ruined an Entire Order of Fish

To understand why this study matters, you need to understand the depth of the hole we've dug for sharks in the public imagination. And that hole has a name: Jaws.

Steven Spielberg's 1975 masterpiece is, by any cinematic standard, a brilliant film. It's also one of the most damaging pieces of animal propaganda ever created. Peter Benchley, who wrote the source novel, spent the last decades of his life as a passionate shark conservationist who openly regretted how his work had demonized an entire group of animals.

The film's legacy is staggering. Recreational shark hunting exploded after its release. Some species declined by over 70% in subsequent decades. But the deeper damage was cultural. Jaws didn't just make people afraid of sharks. It made them incapable of imagining sharks as anything other than biological weapons. The idea that a shark might have preferences, relationships, or social intelligence became as absurd as suggesting your toaster has feelings.

This framing persisted even as evidence to the contrary mounted. Studies on lemon sharks in the 2000s showed they preferred the company of familiar individuals. Research on grey reef sharks found them forming stable social groups. Port Jackson sharks were documented returning to the same partners year after year. The data kept arriving, and the culture kept ignoring it.

We had, in effect, decided what sharks were before we bothered to actually study them. The Exeter study doesn't just challenge that decision — it obliterates it.

03

The Fiji Study: Six Years, 184 Sharks, and a Lot of Patience

The logistics of studying shark social behavior are, to put it mildly, inconvenient. You can't exactly invite a bull shark into a lab, hand it a survey about its friendship preferences, and wait for honest answers. Bull sharks are large, powerful, and perfectly capable of making a researcher's day extremely unpleasant. They also live in murky coastal waters where visibility is often measured in inches rather than feet.

The University of Exeter team, led by Professor Darren Croft, chose the waters off Fiji for their study site — an area where bull sharks aggregate in relatively predictable patterns, giving researchers a recurring window into the same population. Over six years, they identified and tracked 184 individual bull sharks, building what amounts to a comprehensive social database for an entire community of apex predators.

The researchers divided the sharks into three age groups: sub-adults (the teenagers), adults (the prime-of-life crowd), and advanced adults (post-reproductive individuals, the shark equivalent of retirees). This age stratification turned out to be crucial, because as it turns out, shark social behavior changes dramatically across the lifespan — just like it does in humans.

The data collection went far beyond simple proximity tracking. The team analyzed two distinct levels of social interaction. First, they looked at broad association patterns — which sharks were consistently found within one body length of each other. Being within one body length of a bull shark is, for reference, something most sensible organisms actively try to avoid. For another bull shark to willingly maintain that proximity, it suggests a level of tolerance and familiarity that goes well beyond random chance.

Second, and more remarkably, the team documented detailed behavioral interactions between specific individuals. This included "lead-follow" behavior — where one shark consistently takes the lead while another follows — and parallel swimming, where two sharks move in synchronized patterns side by side. These aren't incidental overlaps in movement. These are coordinated social behaviors that require recognition of specific individuals and a willingness to adjust one's own behavior in response to another's.

Think about that for a moment: an animal that most people assume operates on pure instinct is adjusting its behavior based on who it's swimming next to. That's not a reflex. That's a relationship.

04

How Sharks Choose Friends: Active Social Preferences

Here's where the study gets genuinely fascinating — and, if we're being honest, a little humbling.

The Exeter team's analysis revealed that bull sharks display what scientists call "active social preferences." This is a technical way of saying they don't just end up near certain sharks by accident. They seek out specific individuals and avoid others. They are, in the most literal scientific sense, choosing their friends.

This distinction matters enormously. In ecology, there's a constant debate about whether animals that are found together are actually being social or just happen to be in the same place because of shared environmental preferences. Two sharks at the same reef might both be there for the food, not for each other. The Exeter study controlled for this. They accounted for habitat overlap, temporal patterns, and resource distribution. What remained, after stripping away all the environmental noise, was a clear signal: these sharks prefer certain individuals over others, independent of where they are or what they're doing.

The preference patterns were not random. Sharks tended to associate with individuals of similar size — a finding that echoes one of the most robust patterns in human social psychology: we gravitate toward people who are like us. In sociology, this is called "homophily." In the ocean, apparently, it's just called Tuesday.

Size-based association makes intuitive sense. A similarly-sized companion is unlikely to view you as prey (always a plus in a friendship) and is likely to have similar swimming speeds and energy requirements. But the fact that this preference is actively maintained reveals a level of individual recognition and social decision-making that would have seemed laughable to most marine biologists even twenty years ago.

The avoidance patterns were equally telling. Certain shark pairs consistently maintained distance from each other across the entire six-year study period. This wasn't indifference. This was active avoidance — the shark equivalent of seeing someone you don't like at a party and repositioning yourself to the other side of the room. We've all done it. So have bull sharks.

You know that person at work you always seem to end up next to at lunch? That might be homophily. You know the one you always seem to avoid? That's active social avoidance. Bull sharks have both. Welcome to the club.

05

Lead-Follow Behavior: The Body Language of Sharks

If the association data told the researchers who bull sharks prefer, the behavioral data told them how those relationships actually work. And the answer involves a social dynamic that humans will find uncomfortably familiar: some sharks lead, and some sharks follow.

Lead-follow behavior is exactly what it sounds like. In a pair of associated sharks, one consistently takes the front position, setting the direction and pace of movement, while the other follows behind. This isn't random jockeying for position. The same individual tends to lead in repeated interactions with the same partner, suggesting established roles within specific relationships.

In human terms, think of it as the friend who always decides where you're going for dinner. You could object, theoretically, but somehow you always end up at their restaurant of choice. And honestly? The food's usually good, so you don't mind.

The parallel swimming behavior is even more striking. Two sharks moving side by side in synchronized patterns, matching speed and direction, is not something that happens by coincidence. It requires continuous mutual awareness and real-time behavioral adjustment. Each shark is monitoring the other's position and movement and calibrating its own behavior to maintain the parallel formation.

For context, synchronized movement is considered a sophisticated social behavior across the animal kingdom. Schools of fish do it, but they're using a relatively simple set of rules: match your neighbor's speed and direction, maintain a fixed distance. What the bull sharks are doing is different. They're not in a school of hundreds, following algorithmic rules. They're in pairs, making individual choices about a specific partner. It's the difference between being in a crowd that happens to be walking in the same direction and deliberately walking in step with a friend.

This has profound implications for shark cognition. To follow another individual consistently, a shark needs to recognize that individual, remember past interactions, and decide to maintain proximity. To lead, it needs awareness that it's being followed. Neither is a simple reflex. Both require social intelligence that the "mindless predator" narrative cannot accommodate.

When two bull sharks swim in parallel, synchronized and deliberate, they're not executing a program. They're maintaining a relationship. And they're doing it in an environment where a wrong turn can mean getting eaten by something bigger. The stakes are higher than your average friendship, yet they choose to show up anyway.

06

The Popularity Contest: Adult Sharks as Social Hubs

Every social network has its hubs — those individuals who seem to know everyone, who connect disparate groups, who serve as the social glue holding a community together. In human networks, these people are sometimes called "connectors" or "super-connectors." In the bull shark networks of Fiji, they're called adults.

One of the study's most striking findings was that adult bull sharks — those in the prime of their lives, past the awkwardness of sub-adulthood but not yet in the post-reproductive advanced-adult phase — were by far the most socially connected individuals in the population. They had more associations, more diverse contacts, and more frequent interactions than either younger or older sharks.

Adult sharks formed the core of the social network. If you mapped out all the connections between all 184 sharks (and the researchers did exactly this), the adults would be clustered at the center, with sub-adults and advanced adults occupying the periphery. It's a pattern that any sociologist would recognize immediately: the middle generation is the most socially active, while the very young and the very old are more socially isolated.

The reasons for this pattern are probably both practical and biological. Adult bull sharks are at their physical peak — large enough to hold their own in social interactions but still actively engaged in the behaviors (feeding, mating, territorial negotiation) that require social contact. Sub-adults, being smaller and less experienced, may lack the social capital to access the core network. Advanced adults, having passed their reproductive prime, may simply have less motivation to maintain extensive social connections.

But the more interesting question isn't why adults are the most connected. It's what they do with those connections. In social network theory, hubs serve a critical function: they transmit information. In human networks, the person who knows everyone is also the person through whom news, gossip, opportunities, and warnings flow. If bull shark networks function similarly — and there's growing evidence across species that they do — then adult sharks may serve as information conduits for the entire population.

Where's the food? Where's the danger? Where are potential mates? In a species that can't exactly send text messages, social proximity to well-connected individuals might be the next best thing. Being friends with the popular shark isn't just socially rewarding. It might be a survival strategy.

In the shark world, the most popular individual isn't the biggest or the most aggressive. It's the most socially connected adult — the one who knows everyone. High school, it turns out, never really ends. Not even in the ocean.

07

Gender Dynamics: Why Everyone Wants to Hang With the Females

The study also revealed a gender dynamic in shark social networks that will either delight or enrage you, depending on your perspective: both male and female bull sharks preferred to associate with females.

Let that sink in. In a population of 184 bull sharks of both sexes, when given a free choice of social partners, everyone — males and females alike — gravitated toward female companions. Males were more likely to seek out female associates. Females were more likely to maintain bonds with other females. The result was a social network in which female sharks were disproportionately popular.

Now, before you start drawing sweeping conclusions about the inherent social superiority of one sex over another, there are some solid biological reasons for this pattern. Female bull sharks tend to be larger than males. In a species where size-matched associations are the norm, larger females represent desirable social partners for other large individuals. Females may also be less aggressive in social contexts than males, making them safer companions — a consideration that matters quite a lot when your social partner has several rows of serrated teeth.

Males, meanwhile, were found to have more social connections overall, but these connections were less stable and more transient. Male sharks appeared to move between social groups more frequently, maintaining a wider but shallower network. Females maintained fewer but deeper and more consistent associations.

If this is starting to sound familiar, it should. The pattern maps remarkably well onto a widely documented phenomenon in human social psychology: women tend to form fewer, closer, more enduring friendships, while men tend to maintain larger, more diffuse social networks. This isn't a stereotype; it's a robust finding across cultures and age groups. And apparently, across species.

The preference for female associates also makes strategic sense from a mating perspective. For males, proximity to females increases mating opportunities. For females, maintaining bonds with other females may provide safety in numbers — a group of large female bull sharks is not something any predator, or any overly persistent male, is going to hassle lightly.

There may also be a subtler dynamic. In many social species, females are the primary vehicles of cultural knowledge — they live longer, they're more site-faithful, and they maintain more stable social bonds. A female-centered network might serve as a repository of information about safe habitats and productive feeding grounds. Associating with females might literally make you smarter about your environment.

The shark equivalent of "I'd rather hang out with her group" is apparently a universal phenomenon. Evolution keeps arriving at the same social solutions, whether you're a mammal walking on two legs or a cartilaginous fish with three hundred teeth.

08

Shark Social Networks vs. Human Social Networks: An Uncomfortable Comparison

Let's lay the parallels out plainly, because they're more numerous and more uncomfortable than you'd expect.

Humans form social networks characterized by homophily (we prefer people like us), age-stratified connectivity (middle-aged adults are the most socially connected), gendered social patterns (differing network structures between sexes), active preference and avoidance (we choose friends and avoid non-friends), and coordinated social behavior (walking in sync, mirroring body language, lead-follow dynamics in conversation). Bull sharks, according to the Exeter study, do all of these things.

The scale is different, obviously. Human social networks can encompass thousands of individuals; a bull shark's network tops out at maybe a few dozen meaningful connections. But the underlying architecture — the rules governing who connects with whom and how — is startlingly similar.

This shouldn't be entirely surprising. Social network structures are constrained by a relatively small set of variables: the cognitive ability to recognize individuals, the memory to track past interactions, the capacity to adjust behavior based on social context, and the ecological conditions that make sociality beneficial. Humans and bull sharks are both long-lived, cognitively capable predators who operate in complex environments where information sharing and cooperative behavior can provide survival advantages. Given similar pressures, evolution often produces similar solutions.

But here's the part that stings: bull sharks might be more deliberate about their social choices than we are. Humans befriend people who sit near us in class, who share our commute, who happen to be assigned to the same project. Proximity and convenience drive a shocking amount of human social behavior.

Bull sharks don't have offices or commuter trains. Their social environment is three-dimensional, vast, and constantly shifting. You can't passively end up next to the same shark every day because you both take the same bus. If two bull sharks are consistently found together over six years, it's because they're both making a continuous choice to maintain that association.

Your best friend might be your best friend because they sat next to you in ninth grade. A bull shark's best friend is its best friend because, across six years and thousands of square kilometers of open ocean, they kept choosing each other. Every single day. On purpose.

09

What About Other Sharks? Broader Implications for Shark Science

The Exeter study focused on bull sharks, but its implications ripple outward across the entire shark family — all 500-plus species of them.

Bull sharks are already among the more social shark species. They're known to aggregate in specific areas, sometimes in large numbers, and they tolerate closer proximity to other sharks than many species do. If even a notoriously aggressive species like the bull shark has this level of social sophistication, what are we missing in species that are harder to study?

Consider hammerhead sharks, which form schools of hundreds. These aggregations have long been described as simply "gathering at productive feeding sites" — the shark equivalent of a crowded restaurant. But the Exeter study's methodology — analyzing individual-level association patterns and behavioral interactions within groups — suggests that what looks like a random crowd from the outside might be a structured social network from the inside. Maybe those hammerheads aren't just in the same place. Maybe they're there together.

Great white sharks, long considered the ultimate loners, have also started revealing social tendencies. Research at Guadalupe Island has documented white sharks that repeatedly associate with specific individuals across multiple years. Some pairs have been observed engaging in what appears to be social play — following each other, approaching and retreating, adjusting their paths to maintain contact. It's not definitive, but it's suggestive. And in light of the Exeter findings, it's worth taking seriously.

The broader implication is that social behavior in sharks may be the rule rather than the exception. We've been operating under the assumption that most sharks are asocial, and that the few species showing social tendencies are outliers. The Exeter study suggests we should flip that assumption: maybe most sharks are social, and we've just been too biased (and too scared) to notice.

"Contrary to commonly held perceptions of sharks, our study shows they have relatively rich and complex social lives."

That's Professor Darren Croft, speaking with the measured understatement of a scientist who knows he's just upended a popular assumption. Read between the lines and what he's really saying is: everything the public believes about shark behavior is, at best, incomplete and, at worst, flatly wrong.

10

Why This Actually Matters: Conservation, Fisheries, and Rethinking the Predator

Understanding that sharks have social lives isn't just academically interesting. It has immediate, practical consequences for how we manage and protect them.

Current shark conservation strategies are largely population-based. We count how many sharks there are, set catch limits, and hope for the best. But if sharks have social networks — if certain individuals serve as hubs, if social bonds facilitate information transfer, if population-level behavior depends on the structure of social relationships — then a purely numerical approach is dangerously incomplete.

Consider what happens when you remove a social hub from a network. In human organizations, losing a key connector can collapse communication chains, fragment teams, and destroy institutional knowledge. If adult bull sharks serve a similar connective function in their populations, then losing a few well-connected adults could have consequences far beyond the simple reduction in numbers. It could fracture the social network itself, disrupting information flow, destabilizing social bonds, and potentially altering the behavior of the entire population.

This has direct implications for fisheries. Bull sharks are caught commercially and recreationally around the world. If fisheries are disproportionately removing adults — the most socially connected and arguably most ecologically important age class — they could be doing structural damage to shark populations that simple population counts wouldn't detect. A population that looks numerically healthy could be socially shattered.

It also changes how we think about marine protected areas. If bull sharks maintain social bonds with specific individuals and return to specific locations to maintain those bonds, then protecting those locations becomes a matter of preserving not just habitat but social infrastructure. A reef that serves as a social hub for a shark network isn't just a piece of ocean. It's the equivalent of the town square, the cafe where everyone meets, the place where a community is actually a community.

Beyond conservation, the study forces a broader rethinking of what it means to be a predator. We've long associated social intelligence with prey species — the idea being that prey animals need social bonds for collective defense, while predators just need to be good at killing. But bull sharks are apex predators, and they're deeply social. So are orcas, wolves, lions, and chimpanzees. The pattern is clear: being at the top of the food chain doesn't make you a loner. If anything, it makes sociality more valuable, because the stakes of every interaction — cooperative hunting, territorial defense, mate selection — are higher.

The Exeter study, in the end, isn't just a study about sharks. It's a study about the assumptions we bring to science and how those assumptions can blind us to reality for decades. We decided that sharks were mindless, solitary killers, and then we spent fifty years interpreting every piece of evidence through that lens. It took six years of patient, methodical work in the waters off Fiji to show us what was there all along: an animal with a social life that is, in its own way, as complex and deliberate as our own.

The next time you're in the ocean and you see a bull shark, consider the possibility that it's not hunting. It might be on its way to meet a friend. And it might have chosen that friend with more care and intentionality than you chose yours.

So there it is. Bull sharks have friends. They choose those friends carefully. They maintain relationships across years and vast distances. They have social hubs, gender dynamics, age-based connectivity patterns, and coordinated behaviors that mirror our own social structures in ways that are frankly a little eerie.

The ocean's most feared predators are not the mindless killing machines we've spent half a century imagining. They're social animals with rich inner lives and complex relationships. And the fact that it took us this long to figure that out says more about us than it does about them.
