Hedgehogs Can Hear Sounds Humans Can’t — And It Might Save Their Species

March 23, 2026 • 18 min read

The humble hedgehog — that small, spiny creature shuffling through your garden at dusk — has been hiding something extraordinary. For centuries, we thought we knew these animals. We were wrong. A team of scientists just discovered that hedgehogs possess a sensory superpower that puts human hearing to shame, and it might be the key to saving them from the roads that are wiping them out.

This is the story of a tiny animal with enormous ears — in ways we never imagined — and the race to use that discovery before it's too late.

01

The Secret Superpower Hiding in Your Garden

If you have ever watched a hedgehog in the half-light of a summer evening, you have probably noticed the way it pauses, tilts its head, and seems to listen to something just beyond the edge of your perception. You might have assumed it was tracking the rustle of a beetle in the leaves, or the distant padding of a cat on a fence. You were partially right. But the hedgehog was also listening to an entire world of sound that you cannot hear, have never heard, and will never hear.

On March 11, 2026, a landmark paper published in the journal Biology Letters revealed what no one had thought to test in any serious, rigorous way before: the European hedgehog can hear ultrasound. Not faintly. Not marginally. The hedgehog can detect sounds at frequencies of at least 85 kilohertz — more than four times the upper limit of human hearing.

To put that in perspective: you can hear sounds up to roughly 20 kilohertz. Your dog tops out at around 45 kilohertz. Your cat can manage about 64 kilohertz. The hedgehog in your garden hears sounds that all three of you are completely deaf to.

Every night, in gardens and hedgerows across Europe, hedgehogs are listening to a symphony of ultrasonic sound that is utterly invisible to the humans sleeping just meters away.

02

The Discovery That Rewrote What We Know

The research was a collaboration between some of Europe's most respected institutions: the University of Oxford, the Natural History Museum of Denmark, the University of Copenhagen, Aarhus University, and the University of Southern Denmark. It was not a casual side project. It was a carefully designed investigation into a question that had been nagging at a small group of biologists for years: could hedgehogs hear frequencies that we had simply never bothered to test?

The answer came back unambiguous. Using audiometric techniques — essentially, the same type of hearing tests that audiologists use on humans, but adapted for small, spiny, occasionally grumpy mammals — the researchers mapped out the full hearing range of the European hedgehog for the first time.

The results were startling. Hedgehogs can hear sounds starting from around 4 kilohertz, which is comfortably within the human range and roughly equivalent to the highest note on a piano. But where human hearing drops off sharply above 20 kilohertz, the hedgehog's audiogram kept climbing. Their hearing extends to at least 85 kilohertz, with peak sensitivity hovering around 40 kilohertz — firmly in the ultrasonic range, well beyond what any human ear can detect.

This was the first time any study had definitively demonstrated ultrasonic hearing in hedgehogs. The paper represented a genuine paradigm shift in our understanding of a mammal that humans have lived alongside for thousands of years.

"We've shared our gardens with hedgehogs for millennia. We've written poems about them, put them in children's books, built them little houses in our yards. And we didn't know they could hear sounds we can't even imagine."

03

An Engineering Marvel Inside a Tiny Skull

Hearing ultrasound is not simply a matter of having good ears. It requires a fundamentally different mechanical architecture inside the skull. Sound, at its core, is vibration — waves of pressure moving through the air. Low-frequency sounds create long, slow waves that are relatively easy to capture and transmit. High-frequency sounds, and especially ultrasound, create extremely short, rapid waves that demand precision engineering to detect.

The human middle ear contains three tiny bones — the malleus, incus, and stapes — that form a chain connecting the eardrum to the inner ear. When sound hits your eardrum, these bones amplify the vibration and pass it inward. It is an elegant system, but it is tuned for the frequencies that matter most to human survival: the range of speech, the crack of a branch, the growl of a predator. Above 20 kilohertz, the chain of bones is simply too heavy and too loose to vibrate fast enough.

The hedgehog has solved this problem with what can only be described as an act of evolutionary miniaturization. Its middle-ear bones are extraordinarily small and dense — among the most compact of any mammal its size. But the real innovation is structural. The joint between the eardrum and the first bone in the chain (the malleus) is partially fused. In most mammals, this joint is flexible, allowing the bones to swing freely. In the hedgehog, partial fusion makes the entire bone chain stiffer.

A stiffer chain vibrates at higher frequencies. Think of it like the difference between a loose rubber band and a taut guitar string. The loose band wobbles slowly; the taut string hums at a high pitch. By stiffening its middle-ear mechanics, the hedgehog has effectively tuned its hearing apparatus to resonate with ultrasonic frequencies — allowing those tiny, rapid pressure waves to pass efficiently from eardrum to inner ear without losing energy.

This is not a crude adaptation. It is the same fundamental engineering principle found in the ears of echolocating bats — animals that navigate in total darkness by emitting ultrasonic pulses and listening for the returning echoes. The hedgehog's ear, the researchers discovered, shares key structural features with bat ears. Not because hedgehogs echolocate (they don't, as far as we know), but because evolution arrived at the same solution to the same physical problem through two completely independent paths.

Convergent evolution built the same ultrasonic receiver in two wildly different animals. The hedgehog carries bat-grade hearing hardware inside a skull the size of a golf ball.

04

Where the Hedgehog Stands in the Hearing Olympics

To truly appreciate what the hedgehog can do, it helps to see where it falls on the spectrum of mammalian hearing. Sound frequency is measured in hertz (Hz), with one kilohertz (kHz) equaling one thousand vibrations per second. The higher the number, the higher the pitch.

Humans hear from about 20 Hz to 20 kHz. We are generalists — decent across a wide range, but not exceptional at any particular point. By the time we hit middle age, most of us have already lost the top end of our range, and 15 kHz is optimistic for many adults.

Dogs extend to roughly 45 kHz. This is why dog whistles work — they emit sounds in the 23–54 kHz range that dogs can hear but humans cannot. Every time you blow a silent dog whistle, you are operating in the ultrasonic zone.

Cats do better, reaching approximately 64 kHz. This is believed to help them detect the ultrasonic vocalizations of rodents, which communicate at frequencies between 30 and 110 kHz. A cat sitting motionless and staring at a seemingly empty wall may actually be listening to mice having a conversation in ultrasound.

Bats are the acknowledged champions of ultrasonic hearing, with some species detecting frequencies above 200 kHz. This is essential for their echolocation systems, which require emitting and receiving extremely high-frequency sounds to build a sonic picture of their environment.

Dolphins are similarly impressive, using echolocation underwater with hearing that extends to around 150 kHz. Water is a denser medium than air, which changes the physics significantly, but the principle is the same: high-frequency sounds provide more detailed information about small objects.

And then there is the hedgehog, at 85 kHz and counting. It does not echolocate. It does not hunt in total darkness using sonar. It simply toddles through gardens eating slugs. And yet it hears sounds that would be utterly silent to a German Shepherd, a Siamese cat, or any human who has ever lived.

The hedgehog, it turns out, has been listening to a channel that we didn't even know was broadcasting.

Your dog hears twice what you hear. Your cat hears three times what you hear. The hedgehog in your garden hears more than four times what you hear. And it has been doing this in silence, every night, for millions of years.

We named them. We studied them. We thought we understood them. We didn't even know what they were listening to.
05

How Science Missed This for Centuries

It is reasonable to ask how, in an age of genome sequencing and Mars rovers, nobody thought to check whether hedgehogs can hear ultrasound. The answer reveals something uncomfortable about the way science works — and the assumptions that quietly shape what we choose to investigate.

Hedgehogs are not exotic. They are not endangered in the dramatic, headline-grabbing way of tigers or polar bears (though as we will see, their situation is dire). They do not live in remote jungles or deep-sea trenches. They live in gardens. They eat cat food left on porches. They get stuck in drain grates. They are, in the popular imagination, charming but unremarkable.

And this is precisely the problem. Scientific attention, like media attention, follows novelty and spectacle. Billions of research dollars flow toward charismatic megafauna, toward species that are visually dramatic or ecologically extreme. The hedgehog, with its shuffling gait and its habit of curling into a ball when frightened, does not generate the same urgency.

Previous studies of hedgehog hearing were limited in scope and technology. Early audiometric work in the mid-twentieth century tested hedgehog hearing only within the frequency ranges that researchers expected to find relevant — roughly the same range as human hearing, perhaps a little higher. Nobody pushed the equipment to 85 kHz because nobody had a reason to suspect the results would be interesting.

There was also a taxonomic assumption at work. Hedgehogs belong to the order Eulipotyphla, which includes shrews and moles. Shrews are known to have relatively poor hearing. The assumption — never rigorously tested — was that hedgehogs would be similar. They're small insectivores. How fancy could their ears be?

Very fancy, as it turns out. The 2026 paper did not just discover ultrasonic hearing; it demolished a set of assumptions that had been quietly steering research away from the question for decades. The hedgehog was hiding its superpower in plain sight, and we simply never thought to look.

Science is excellent at finding answers. It is less good at questioning the questions it chooses not to ask. The hedgehog's secret was not buried in a remote cave. It was in every garden in Europe, waiting for someone to bring the right equipment.

06

The Hedgehog Crisis: Death on Four Lanes

Here is where the story turns from fascinating to urgent. Because while scientists were discovering what hedgehogs can hear, the hedgehogs themselves were dying in numbers that should alarm anyone who cares about the natural world.

The European hedgehog is in serious trouble. Populations across Western Europe have been declining for decades, and in some regions, the collapse has been catastrophic. In the United Kingdom — a country so fond of hedgehogs that they appear on postage stamps and inspire dedicated charities — the population has fallen from an estimated 30 million in the 1950s to fewer than one million today. That is a decline of more than 95 percent within living memory.

The causes are multiple: loss of hedgerows and wild habitat to intensive agriculture, the use of pesticides that kill the insects hedgehogs depend on, the tidying of gardens into sterile lawns, impermeable fencing that fragments their territory, and predation by badgers in some areas. But one cause stands out for its sheer, brutal simplicity: roads.

Road traffic kills an enormous number of hedgehogs every year. Estimates vary by country, but the scale is staggering. In the UK alone, surveys suggest that hundreds of thousands of hedgehogs are killed on roads annually. In the Netherlands, Germany, France, and Scandinavia, the numbers are similarly grim. Roads slice through hedgehog habitat like knife wounds, and hedgehogs have no evolutionary defense against a two-ton vehicle traveling at sixty miles per hour.

When a hedgehog encounters a threat, its instinct is to curl into a ball. This strategy is devastatingly effective against foxes and dogs. Against a car tire, it is a death sentence. The hedgehog's armor, perfected over millions of years of evolution, is useless against the one predator it was never designed to face.

And the timing could not be worse. Hedgehogs are most active at night, precisely when visibility is lowest and roads are darkest. They move slowly. They freeze when they sense danger. Everything about their behavior — every survival instinct honed over sixty million years of evolution — conspires to place them in the path of oncoming traffic at the worst possible moment.

The hedgehog's greatest evolutionary achievement — its spiny armor — is also its greatest vulnerability on modern roads. A defense that defeated predators for sixty million years is meaningless against a moving car.

07

The Solution We Could Never Have Designed Without This Discovery

This is where the discovery of ultrasonic hearing transforms from a curiosity into something that could genuinely save a species.

For years, conservationists have struggled with the road-kill problem. Wildlife crossings — tunnels and bridges that allow animals to pass under or over roads — work well for larger animals like deer and badgers, but they are expensive, require careful placement, and hedgehogs do not reliably use them. Reduced speed limits help, but hedgehogs are active at night when enforcement is difficult and drivers are least attentive. Warning signs are largely ignored.

The researchers behind the Biology Letters paper have proposed something radically different: ultrasonic repellent devices. The concept is elegant in its simplicity. Small, inexpensive emitters could be placed along road verges or integrated into roadside infrastructure. These devices would broadcast sound at frequencies in the hedgehog's peak sensitivity range — around 40 kHz — creating an invisible acoustic barrier that hedgehogs would find unpleasant or alarming, encouraging them to turn away before reaching the road surface.

The beauty of this approach lies in what makes it possible: the frequency gap between hedgehog hearing and human hearing. At 40 kHz, the repellent signal would be completely inaudible to humans. You could walk along a road lined with these devices and hear nothing. Your dog, with its upper limit of around 45 kHz, would barely register the sound or not hear it at all. Your cat might detect a faint whine at the edge of perception. But the hedgehog — with its peak sensitivity precisely tuned to 40 kHz — would hear it loud and clear.

This is not a theoretical pipe dream. Ultrasonic deterrents already exist for other purposes. They are used to repel rodents from buildings, to keep deer away from airport runways, and to deter teenagers from loitering (a controversial application called the "Mosquito" device, which emits a high-pitched sound that younger ears can detect but older ears cannot). The technology is proven. What was missing was the knowledge that hedgehogs could hear ultrasound at all.

Now, with the audiometric data from the 2026 study, engineers can design repellent systems specifically calibrated to the hedgehog's hearing profile. The frequency, volume, and pattern of the signal can be optimized to be maximally effective for hedgehogs while remaining completely silent to humans and domestic pets. It is, in effect, a force field that only hedgehogs can perceive.

Imagine a world where every dangerous road edge hums with a silent warning that only hedgehogs can hear. The cars keep moving. The humans hear nothing. The dogs and cats are undisturbed. And the hedgehogs, warned by a sound designed just for them, turn back to the safety of the hedge.

08

Silent Guardians: The Sounds We Cannot Hear, Protecting the Animals We Love

There is something deeply poetic about the idea that we might save hedgehogs using a sense that we ourselves do not possess. The solution would operate entirely outside human perception — a conservation tool that works in the silence beyond our hearing, in the invisible acoustic landscape that hedgehogs inhabit and we do not.

We tend to think of conservation as something loud: protests, campaigns, legislation, the roar of public opinion. And those things matter enormously. But there is also a quieter kind of conservation — the kind that works in frequencies we cannot detect, in hours when we are asleep, in the liminal spaces between our world and theirs.

The hedgehog's ultrasonic hearing is a reminder that the animals around us experience reality in ways that are fundamentally different from our own. We share the same gardens, the same hedgerows, the same patches of moonlight. But the hedgehog hears a world that is richer, higher, and more textured than anything our ears can access. When we step outside on a summer evening and hear crickets and distant traffic, the hedgehog is hearing all of that plus an entire register of sound that sits above our ceiling of perception like a hidden floor in a building we thought we knew.

This is not merely a factoid. It is a philosophical shift. If a common garden animal — one of the most familiar creatures in Europe — was hiding a sense that we never detected until 2026, what else are we missing? How many other animals are we living alongside without truly understanding what they experience?

The ultrasonic road repellent, if it works as hoped, will be a triumph of science applied with care and precision. But it will also be something more: an acknowledgment that the best way to protect another species is sometimes to meet them on their own terms, in their own sensory world, using the capabilities that evolution gave them rather than the ones it gave us.

The most powerful conservation tool for hedgehogs might be a sound that no human will ever hear. There is something beautiful about that — a silent covenant between two species, one of which will never know it is being protected, and the other of which will never hear the protection happening.

We cannot hear the frequency that will save them. They will never understand the science that designed it. And somewhere in that gap between two kinds of knowing, a species might survive.
09

What Else Don't We Know? The Hidden Senses of Common Animals

The hedgehog discovery is not an isolated case. It is part of a growing pattern in biology that suggests we have been systematically underestimating the sensory capabilities of the animals we think we know best.

In 2023, researchers discovered that pigeons — those ubiquitous, much-maligned city birds — can detect the Earth's magnetic field using iron-rich cells in their beaks, giving them a built-in compass that allows them to navigate across continents. We had suspected magnetic sensing in birds for decades, but the mechanism was only recently confirmed. Pigeons, it turns out, carry a piece of navigational technology in their faces that human engineers have been trying to miniaturize for years.

Elephants communicate using infrasound — sounds so low in frequency that they are below the range of human hearing. These rumbling calls can travel through the ground for distances of up to ten kilometers, and elephants detect them through their feet. An elephant standing in apparent silence may be having a conversation with another elephant six miles away, using a channel that is completely invisible to every human in between.

Sharks detect electrical fields generated by the muscle contractions of their prey, using specialized organs called ampullae of Lorenzini. This electroreception is so sensitive that a shark can detect the electrical field of a flounder buried in sand from half a meter away. The fish is invisible, motionless, silent, and scentless. The shark finds it anyway, using a sense that humans do not possess and cannot replicate without electronic equipment.

Mantis shrimps see polarized light and have sixteen types of color receptors (humans have three). Pit vipers see infrared radiation, building thermal images of their environment that overlay their visual world like a night-vision display. Migratory birds may literally see magnetic field lines superimposed on their visual landscape, guiding them along routes that span hemispheres.

Every one of these discoveries overturned an assumption. Every one of them revealed that a familiar animal was doing something extraordinary that we had simply failed to notice. And every one of them raises the same humbling question: what else are we missing?

The hedgehog joins this list as a particularly striking example, precisely because it is so ordinary. It is not a deep-sea creature from an alien environment. It is not a tropical bird from an unexplored rainforest. It is an animal that lives in suburban gardens, that eats from pet bowls left on back steps, that gets featured on greeting cards and tea towels. And it was hiding a sensory superpower that nobody discovered until 2026.

If we missed ultrasonic hearing in an animal that literally lives in our gardens, the honest question is not "what else haven't we found?" but "how long is the list of things we haven't thought to look for?"

10

The Bigger Picture: How Little We Understand About the Animals Around Us

We live in an age of extraordinary scientific capability. We can sequence a genome in hours, image individual neurons firing in a living brain, and detect gravitational waves from colliding black holes a billion light-years away. And yet, until a few weeks ago, we did not know that the hedgehog in the garden can hear ultrasound.

This is not a failure of technology. The equipment to test hedgehog hearing at ultrasonic frequencies has existed for decades. This is a failure of imagination — a collective blind spot born from the assumption that familiar animals must be fully understood animals. We looked at the hedgehog and saw a small, simple creature. We did not think to ask what it was hearing, because we assumed it was hearing roughly what we were hearing.

That assumption was wrong. And the implications extend far beyond hedgehogs.

There are approximately 6,400 known species of mammals on Earth. The hearing ranges of the vast majority have never been rigorously tested. We have detailed audiograms for perhaps a few hundred species — mostly domesticated animals, lab animals, and a handful of species that attracted attention because of echolocation or other obvious acoustic behaviors. For the rest, we are guessing. We are extrapolating from related species, assuming that if one shrew has poor hearing, all members of the same order must be similar.

The hedgehog study just proved that this approach is unreliable. If a common European insectivore can hear ultrasound using a mechanism that parallels bat ears, then our assumptions about the sensory worlds of other "ordinary" animals may be equally wrong. What about badgers? Rabbits? Squirrels? Foxes? How many garden animals are we sharing space with whose perceptual worlds we have never properly explored?

There is an emerging field called sensory ecology — the study of how animals perceive their environment and how those perceptions shape their behavior, evolution, and survival. Ed Yong's Pulitzer Prize-winning book An Immense World, published in 2022, brought this field to popular attention, revealing the astonishing sensory diversity of the animal kingdom. The hedgehog discovery is a perfect case study for this approach: an animal's hidden sense, once understood, immediately suggests new conservation strategies that would have been impossible without that knowledge.

This is the real lesson of the hedgehog's secret superpower. It is not just about hedgehogs, or ultrasound, or road safety. It is about the depth of our ignorance regarding the creatures that share our world. Every garden, every hedgerow, every patch of urban wilderness is teeming with animals whose sensory experiences are richer, stranger, and more complex than we have ever bothered to discover.

We are surrounded by mysteries. The hedgehog was one of them. It will not be the last.

The hedgehog did not evolve ultrasonic hearing for our benefit. It did not develop its extraordinary ears so that we could design clever road repellents. It simply lived, listened, and survived, for sixty million years, in a world of sound that we were never invited to hear. The least we can do is use what we've learned to return the favor.

Sixty million years of evolution gave the hedgehog ears that can hear the invisible. Two centuries of industrialization gave us roads that are killing them. One scientific paper might finally let us bridge the gap — with a sound that only they can hear.

The hedgehog's story is not over. If anything, it is just beginning a new chapter — one in which a secret hidden inside a tiny skull might become the key to survival on a planet reshaped by human hands. The ultrasonic road repellent is still in development. The population decline is still accelerating. The clock is ticking.

But for the first time, we have something we did not have before: knowledge. We know what hedgehogs can hear. We know how their ears work. We know that we can speak to them in a language that only they understand. And in conservation, knowledge is the difference between watching a species fade away and having the tools to pull it back from the edge.

Tonight, somewhere in Europe, a hedgehog will emerge from beneath a garden shed. It will pause at the edge of a lawn, tilt its head, and listen. It will hear the rustle of leaves, the chirp of insects, and a vast, shimmering landscape of ultrasonic sound that paints its world in frequencies we will never perceive. And then, if we do our job right, it will hear one more thing: a faint, high-pitched warning from the road's edge, designed by scientists it will never meet, broadcast in a frequency it was born to hear, saying simply — not this way. Go back. You're safe here.

It won't know where the sound came from. It won't understand why. It will just turn around, shuffle back through the gap in the fence, and disappear into the darkness of the garden. Alive. Safe. Listening to a world we cannot hear.

And that, perhaps, is the most useless and interesting thing of all.
