"Einstein Failed Math" Is a Myth — And Other Famous Facts That Are Completely Wrong

📅 April 15, 2026 ⏱ 6 min read ✦ UselesslyInteresting

In this article

  1. Einstein: The Myth of the Failed Genius
  2. Napoleon: The Height of a Misunderstanding
  3. More Confident Nonsense About Famous People
  4. Why We Believe Wrong Things So Firmly

There's a particular category of "facts" that everyone knows — the kind that get shared as inspirational memes and cited in graduation speeches. Einstein failed school. Napoleon was tiny. We only use 10% of our brains. Each one is repeated with absolute confidence by millions of people. Each one is wrong. Specifically, traceable-error, we-know-how-this-happened wrong.

Einstein: The Myth of the Failed Genius

Albert Einstein did not fail mathematics. He did not fail school. He was, by all available historical evidence, an exceptionally good student in subjects he cared about. At age twelve he was working through an entire algebra textbook over a summer holiday for fun. By fifteen he had independently mastered calculus.

Where did the myth come from? Probably a combination of two things. First, Einstein did fail the entrance exam to the Swiss Federal Polytechnic in 1895 — but he was two years younger than the other applicants and his French (a required subject) was poor. He passed the math and physics sections with exceptional marks. Second, Switzerland switched its grading system in 1900. In the old system, 6 was the lowest grade and 1 the highest. In the new system, it reversed. A historical record showing Einstein getting "1s" and "2s" in his early schooling looks like failure in the modern scale — but it was the highest possible grade in the scale of the time.

Einstein himself addressed the myth directly. When shown a report claiming he had failed math in school, he reportedly laughed and said "Before I was fifteen, I had mastered differential and integral calculus." The myth persists because it's a better story. A genius who struggled in school is inspiring. A genius who was always brilliant is just intimidating.

✦ Quick fact

The "we only use 10% of our brains" myth has no known single origin, but neuroscientists believe it may have grown from a misquote of psychologist William James, who wrote that humans fail to use their full mental potential — a psychological observation about motivation, not a neurological claim about brain activity. Brain scans show the entire brain is active during normal function. No 90% sits idle.

Napoleon: The Height of a Misunderstanding

Napoleon Bonaparte stood approximately 5 feet 6 inches (168 cm) tall — which was slightly above average for a French man of his era. He was not short. The "short Napoleon" myth is one of the most successful pieces of wartime propaganda in history, originating with British caricaturist James Gillray, whose cartoons depicted Napoleon as a tiny, furious figure to mock and diminish him.

The confusion was amplified by a genuine measurement discrepancy. French inches were longer than British inches at the time. Napoleon's official military record measured him at 5 pieds 2 pouces — which in French units converts to about 5'7" in modern measurements. British observers, misreading this as "5 foot 2 inches" in their own units, concluded he was tiny. He was not. His nickname among troops was "le petit caporal" — "the little corporal" — which was an affectionate term referring to his rank and approachability, not his height.

More Confident Nonsense About Famous People

Isaac Newton was not hit on the head by an apple. The apple story is real — he did describe watching an apple fall in a garden as prompting thoughts about gravity — but accounts of it striking him were embellishments added later. Walt Disney was not cryogenically frozen. He died of lung cancer in December 1966 and was cremated two days later. The first recorded human cryonic preservation took place in January 1967, a month after his death. Van Gogh did not cut off his entire ear. He cut off the lower portion of his left earlobe. A significant portion of the ear remained. The full-ear version makes a better story and has completely replaced the accurate one in popular imagination.

The pattern is consistent: the dramatic version survives; the accurate version doesn't. Short Napoleon is funnier than average-height Napoleon. Ear-cutting Van Gogh is more tragic than earlobe-nicking Van Gogh. Failed-math Einstein is more inspiring than always-brilliant Einstein. Our collective mythology about famous people is essentially a process of editing for maximum emotional impact, with accuracy as a secondary concern.

✦ Quick fact

Abraham Lincoln was, by contrast, genuinely extraordinary in the physical dimension myths often get wrong. He stood 6 feet 4 inches (193 cm) — exceptionally tall for the 19th century. He was also an accomplished wrestler, going undefeated in approximately 300 matches over his career. The one documented loss remains disputed by historians. Lincoln was exactly as physically imposing as the legends say, which makes him the rare famous figure whose popular image is, if anything, an understatement.

Why We Believe Wrong Things So Firmly

Psychologists call it the illusory truth effect: repeated exposure to a statement increases our belief in it, regardless of whether it's true. A claim heard ten times feels more credible than a claim heard once, even if the person hearing it has no memory of the previous exposures. This is why myths that spread widely become almost impossible to dislodge — each retelling feels like corroboration.

There's also a confirmation bias at work: we remember information that fits existing narratives and forget information that complicates them. "Einstein was a mediocre student who overcame failure" fits the narrative of resilience. "Einstein was always a prodigy who also happened to be a kind and funny person" is accurate, but it doesn't fit a satisfying story arc, so our brains deprioritize it.

The most useful takeaway might be this: the facts you're most confident about — the ones that feel like settled, obvious common knowledge — are precisely the ones most worth checking. Myths don't persist because they're obscure. They persist because they feel true. And feeling true is, unfortunately, not the same thing as being true.
